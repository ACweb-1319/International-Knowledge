Understanding IFRS vs GAAP – The Global Accounting Standards

Explore Standards

Why Global Standards Matter

What is IFRS?

International Financial Reporting Standards are a set of accounting rules currently used by public companies in more than 140 jurisdictions. They provide a global framework for how companies prepare and disclose their financial statements.

What is GAAP?

Generally Accepted Accounting Principles are the standard framework of guidelines for financial accounting used in any given jurisdiction, but generally refers to the standards used in the United States.

Global Importance

Understanding both systems is crucial for global investors, multinational corporations, and finance professionals aiming to navigate the complexities of international trade and investment.

IFRS Highlights

  • Definition: Global accounting standards designed to provide universally comparable financial information.
  • Who uses it: Over 144 jurisdictions worldwide, notably the EU, Australia, and Canada.
  • Key Principles: Principles-based, prioritizing the economic reality over strict rules.
  • Advantages: Enhances international comparability, improves capital flow, and reduces reporting costs.
🌍

Global Reach

The standard of choice for the majority of the developed world.

GAAP Highlights

  • Definition: U.S. standards designed to ensure consistency and comparability within the country.
  • Who uses it: United States public and private entities, managed by FASB.
  • Core Rules: Rules-based, providing specific guidelines and procedures for various scenarios.
  • Advantages: Reduces ambiguity, provides detailed industry-specific guidelines.
🏛️

US Market Focus

Rigorous, highly-detailed rules catering to complex US industries.

Head-to-Head Comparison

Feature IFRS (International) US GAAP (United States)
Approach Principles-based Rules-based
Inventory Valuation LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) is prohibited. Allows FIFO and weighted average. Allows LIFO, FIFO, and weighted average methods.
Inventory Reversal Allows reversal of inventory write-downs if criteria are met. Prohibits reversal of inventory write-downs.
Revenue Recognition General guidelines for when risks and rewards transfer to the buyer. Complex, highly detailed, industry-specific revenue recognition rules.
Development Costs Can be capitalized if specific technical and economic criteria are met. Must be expensed as incurred (with very few software exceptions).
